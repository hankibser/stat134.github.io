Prerequisites:

Mastery of the material in Appendices 1-4 of the text, fluency with calculus (derivatives and integrals) in two variables, and – these are crucial – clear logical reasoning and strong problem-solving skills. Test yourself on some practice problems.

Textbook:

Probability, Jim Pitman

Homework:

  • Assignments are posted here and in the calendar below. They are due each Wednesday in section, by 20 minutes after the hour. (i.e. If your section starts at 3 pm, you must hand in your homework by 3.20 pm at the latest).

  • Papers will be graded on a 0/1 scale. Each assignment will consist of 8 problems. A good attempt at 6 or more problems will receive the score of 1; anything else will get 0. Course staff will define what constitutes a "good attempt." We will be looking for reasoning and detailed work shown – it is assumed that you will show work or provide reasoning whether or not the question asks for it.

  • No late homeworks will be accepted and cheating will not be tolerated. While you are encouraged to work with other people, you must write up your own solutions.

  • Your two lowest homework scores will be dropped in the computation of your overall homework score.

Quizzes:

There will be four quizzes during the term, in section, on dates listed in the calendar below. Quizzes will not be given at any other time. You must take your quiz in the section in which you are enrolled. Your lowest quiz score will be dropped in the computation of your overall quiz score.

Exams:

  • Midterm: Thursday March 2nd, in lecture.

  • Final: Wednesday May 10th, 11:30 a.m. - 2:30 p.m. (Exam Group 10)

Grading:

  • Maximum Of:

  • 10% Homework, 25% Quizzes, 25% Midterm, 40% Final

  • 10% Homework, 25% Quizzes, 65% Final

  • Note that the above grading system implies that the Final clobbers the Midterm. Additionally, your two lowest homework scores and your lowest quiz score will be dropped.
